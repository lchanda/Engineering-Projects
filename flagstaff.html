Welcome to Flagstaff, Arizona

Located amidst the stunning landscapes of northern Arizona, Flagstaff offers a unique blend of natural beauty and vibrant culture. Surrounded by the Coconino National Forest and the majestic San Francisco Peaks, this mountain town boasts four distinct seasons, attracting outdoor enthusiasts year-round for hiking, skiing, and exploring. Beyond its scenic allure, Flagstaff is a hub of scientific discovery, home to the Lowell Observatory where Pluto was discovered, and Northern Arizona University, adding a youthful energy to the city. Its historic downtown, with its Route 66 charm and lively arts scene, provides a welcoming atmosphere, making Flagstaff a captivating destination that seamlessly combines adventure and intellectual curiosity.

Popular Spots to Visit

Walnut Canyon National Monument

Walnut Canyon National Monument, situated near Flagstaff, Arizona, protects ancient cliff dwellings built by the Sinagua people between 1100 and 1250 AD. The canyon, carved by Walnut Creek over millions of years, plunges 400 feet deep, revealing diverse geological layers and creating natural alcoves where the Sinagua constructed their homes. Established in 1915 by President Woodrow Wilson to preserve these remarkable archaeological sites, the monument offers visitors a glimpse into the lives of the Sinagua, who were skilled farmers and traders adapted to the relatively dry environment. The name "Sinagua," meaning "without water" in Spanish, reflects their ability to thrive in this region. Wupatki National Monument

Wupatki National Monument, located in north-central Arizona near Flagstaff, is a captivating landscape dotted with the well-preserved ancestral Puebloan ruins of the Sinagua people and their neighbors. Established in 1924, the monument spans over 35,000 acres, showcasing a unique period of cultural convergence in the high desert.   The most significant construction period at Wupatki began after the eruption of nearby Sunset Crater Volcano around 1085 CE. The resulting volcanic ash enriched the soil, making the area more favorable for dryland farming and attracting a diverse population. People from different cultural groups, primarily the Sinagua, but also the Cohonina and Kayenta Ancestral Puebloans, migrated to the area and built numerous settlements.   The monument's namesake, Wupatki Pueblo, stands as a testament to this era. Meaning "Tall House" in the Hopi language, it is a multi-story structure containing over 100 rooms, making it the largest building within a 50-mile radius during its time. This impressive pueblo also features a community room and a unique masonry ballcourt, the northernmost example of such a structure in North America, hinting at connections with cultures further south.

Lowell Observatory

Founded in 1894 by astronomer Percival Lowell, its initial purpose was to study Mars and the possibility of intelligent life there. While that specific quest didn't yield the anticipated results, the observatory quickly became a pivotal center for astronomical research.
